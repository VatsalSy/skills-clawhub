# Shanghai Culture Guide

## Core Mindset
- Shanghai culture combines commercial pragmatism with strong local identity.
- Professional reliability and social respect are both important.
- Politeness, punctuality, and context awareness go a long way.

## Language Reality
- Mandarin is the default in work and services.
- Shanghainese exists in family and local contexts but is not required for newcomers.
- Basic Mandarin phrases improve daily life quickly.

## Social Etiquette
- Greet people respectfully, especially in first meetings.
- Exchange contact details early, usually through mobile apps.
- Avoid public confrontation; give feedback privately when possible.
- Modesty in self-presentation is generally better than hard self-promotion.

## Business Interaction Norms
- Relationship trust builds through consistency over time.
- Decisions may happen after informal alignment, not only in meetings.
- Send clear follow-up notes with owners and deadlines.
- Be precise on commitments; missed promises damage credibility fast.

## Dining Etiquette
- Group meals are common for relationship building.
- Let the host guide seating and first toasts.
- Share dishes; ordering only individual plates can feel distant.
- If you do not drink alcohol, decline politely and consistently.

## Holidays And Rhythm
- Lunar New Year causes major travel and business slowdown.
- Golden Week periods create heavy crowding and booking pressure.
- Plan important paperwork and hiring around holiday calendars.

## Sensitive Topics
- Keep politics and geopolitical debates out of early relationships.
- Avoid stereotyping local or national identity.
- Ask questions with curiosity, not comparison framing.

## Everyday Behavior Tips
- Queue discipline is improving but varies by location.
- Keep voice volume moderate in public indoor spaces.
- Respect shared residential rules on noise and deliveries.
- Learn basic neighborhood routines; this reduces daily friction.
